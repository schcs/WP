Lemma 2.19

$\newcommand{\sym}[1]{{\rm Sym}\,#1}$
$\newcommand{\fix}[2]{{\rm Fix}_{#1}(#2)}$
The problem was reported by Joris Buloron.

Unfortunately, there is an oversight in the proof of the Lemma, and so the assertion of the lemma may not be true for certain infinite permutation groups. A correct version of the lemma is the following.

Lemma 2.19. If $G$ is transitive on $\Omega$, $\omega\in\Omega$, and $N$ is a normal subgroup of $G$, then $G_\omega\leq N_G(N_\omega)$ and $\omega N_G(N_\omega)\subseteq\fix\Omega{N_\omega}$. If $N_\omega$ has no infinite ascending chain
\begin{equation}\label{eq:chain}
N_\omega < N_{\omega_1} < N_{\omega_2} < \cdots
\end{equation}
of stabilisers (with $\omega,\omega_i\in\Omega$ for all $i\geq 1)$, then $\omega N_G(N_\omega)=\mbox{Fix}_\Omega(N_\omega)$. In this case, $|\fix\Omega{N_\omega}|=|N_G(N_\omega):G_\omega|$ and $\fix\Omega{N_\omega}$ is a block for $G$.

Proof. Since $N_\omega=N\cap G_\omega$ and $N$ is normal in $G$, it follows that $N_\omega$ is normal in $G_\omega$, and so $G_\omega\leq N_G (N_\omega)$. The claim that $\omega N_G(N_\omega)\subseteq\fix\Omega{N_\omega}$ can be proved following the argument in the book.

Unfortunately, the proof that $\fix\Omega{N_\omega}\subseteq \omega N_G(N_\omega)$ is not correct. Let us review our argument. If $\omega_1\in\fix\Omega{N_\omega}$, then there is some $g\in G$ such that $\omega_1=\omega g$. Furthermore, we have, for all $n\in N_\omega$, that $\omega gn=\omega g$, and, as $N$ is normal in $G$, $gng^{-1}\in G_\omega\cap N=N_\omega$. As this holds for all $n\in N_\omega$, this implies that $g N_\omega g^{-1}\leq N_\omega$, and so $N_\omega\leq g^{-1} N_\omega g$ (but it may not imply, as we claimed, that $N_\omega =g^{-1} N_\omega g$).

Assume that the proper containment $N_\omega < g^{-1}N_\omega g$ holds. Then we obtain that
$$
N_\omega <g^{-1}N_\omega g<g^{-1}(g^{-1}N_\omega g)g=g^{-2} N_\omega g^{2}.
$$
Calculating similarly the conjugate $g^{-k} N_\omega g^{k}$ for $k\geq 1$, we obtain the infinite ascending chain of subgroups
$$
N_\omega< g^{-1}N_\omega g< g^{-2} N_\omega g^{2}<\cdots< g^{-k} N_\omega g^k<\cdots.
$$
Since $g^{-k} N_\omega g^{k}=N_{\omega_k}$ where $\omega_k=\omega g^k$, we obtain the chain of stabilisers as in \eqref{eq:chain}.
Assuming that no such chain exists,  the rest of the assertions of the lemma can be verified as presented in the book. $\Box$

Note that the condition that $N_\omega$ has no infinite chain of stabilisers is trivially satisfied if, for instance, $N$ (or $G$) is a finite group or if $G$ is a torsion group.

It is possible in a group $G$ and for a subgroup $H\leq G$ that $H<H^g$ for some $g$; the this discussion of MathOverFlow cites several examples.

The first sentence of Section 2.6 should read as follows.

Lemma 2.19 shows us that, for $H \leq G_\omega$ , the normaliser $N_G(H)$ is transitive on the set $\fix \Omega H$ when $H = N_\omega$ , for some normal subgroup
$N$ of $G$ that has no descending chain of stabilisers.

Corollary 2.21 needs to be modified.

Corollary 2.21. Let $G$ be a group acting primitively on $\Omega$ and let $N$ be a normal subgroup of $G$ which is not contained in the kernel of the action. Then $N$ is transitive.n particular, every non-trivial normal subgroup of a primitive permutation group is transitive. Furthermore, if $N_\omega$ has no infinite ascending chain of stabilisers as in \eqref{eq:chain}, then either $N$ induces a regular permutation group on $\Omega$ or $\fix \Omega{N_\omega}=\{\omega\}$ for each $\omega\in\Omega$.

Also, in Theorem 3.2, statement (ii) will need to be changed:

Theorem 3.2(ii). $\omega C_{\sym\Omega}(G)=\omega N_G(G_\omega)\subseteq \fix\Omega{G_\omega}$

The proof of parts (i)-(iii) of Theorem 3.2 can be done as in the book. However, in the proof of part (iv), more care need to be taken. Assume that $C_{\sym\Omega}(G)$ is transitive. Then, $\omega C_{\sym\Omega}(G)=\Omega$, and hence part (ii) implies that $\fix\Omega {G_\omega}=\Omega$. That is, $G$ is regular. Suppose now that $G$ is regular. Then $G_\omega=1$, and so there is no infinite descending chain of subgroups in $G_\omega$. Applying Lemma 2.19 with $N=G$, we obtain that $\fix\Omega{G_\omega}=\omega N_G(G_\omega)=\omega C_{\sym\Omega}(G)$. Since $G$ is regular, $\fix\Omega{G_\omega}=\Omega$, and hence $C_{\sym\Omega}(G)$ is transitive.
